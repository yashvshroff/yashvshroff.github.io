Career Overview

Wall Street is dominated by trailblazers. Everyday, analysts are researching new methods to optimize high frequency trading and striving to change the way the world looks at investing. Likewise, the innovative realm of mathematical computer science controls the 21st century, leaving nothing but imagination to spur endless technological possibilities.

Combining the trailblazer mindset of a data driven analyst with the limitless innovation through computer science creates the “Rocket Scientists of Wall Street”: Computational Quantitative Analysts (Quants)

I chose the field of Computational Quantitative Analysis because I have extensive knowledge in the fields of computational science and business finance from my professional experiences. I have practiced the industry's skills through relevant coursework and multiple enhancement projects. My rounded involvement in FBLA has spurred me to become an Analyst by letting me experience all four concentrations of the field: development, risk management, trading, and research. By combining my knowledge in business with my solid technical background, I know I can be a successful Computational Quantitative Analyst.

Let's break down Computational Quantitative Analysis:

General Description of a Quant:

A quant, quantitative analyst, develops and implements complex mathematical models to come up with profitable investment strategies for hedge funds and other financial industries. A quant aims to reduce the risk of an investment and generate profits in a market, acting as both a logician and speculator. They use traditional statistical models, such as null hypotenuse testing, to forecast market movements. They consistently develop hypotheses for how data sets affect investment assets and stocks. They also analyze the capacity of a data set, determining whether an investment plan should remain the same, or change, based on the current market. A quant can specialize in any of the following fields: Front Office, Back Office, Risk Management, Model Verification, and Algorithm Researcher. Quants are always competing against each other to be the first to find innovative ways to predict the market: ex. Use Satellite Imagery to look over the production of branded fruit farms and determine their market value through analyzing the abundance, health, and length of the crops from the satellite images.

Skills Required/Obstacles

A quantitative analyst is usually considered a jack-of-all-trades, as they need to specialize their skills in mathematics, computer science, business, and finance.

Quants need to be Competent in various mathematical fields

  • Differential and Stochastic Calculus
  • Numerical Linear Algebra and Differential Equations
  • Probability and Statistics
  • Game Theory

Quants need to understand different computer science languages and programs

  • Big Data modeling and Object Oriented programing
  • Mathematical algorithm and model graphing languages: MatLab and SAS
  • Machine Learning and Data Mining utilizing Python Programming and C++
  • Ability to innovate prediction and classification computer programs and algorithms

Quants require keen business management skills

  • Analytical Problem Solving to figure out unique financial situations
  • Working under pressure to make spontaneous financial decisions
  • Persistence to work on very challenging projects without supervision
  • Ability to Concentrate on computer code and data for long hours

Quants require fundamental competence in Financial Practices

  • Ability to design appealing portfolios
  • Plan portfolios utilizing equity and interest rate derivatives to their benefit
  • Competence of both Systematic (manual) and Discretionary (rule-based) trading
  • Ability to understand financial trends through financial models

Obstacles

A quant’s main obstacle is to maintain a healthy work environment. Finance jobs are infamous for gruesome working hours, including for quants who average 60-90 hour work weeks. As they compete to be the first to create innovative financial predictions, their work hours greatly increase. In addition, constantly staring at numbers to figure out data patterns and creating hundreds of mathematical models can be overwhelming. Successful quants must be able to efficiently work under this pressure.

Education Requirements

A quant starts their career off with an undergraduate degree at a reputable college in either Computer Science, Mathematics, Hardware/Electrical Engineering, or Theoretical Physics. After receiving an undergraduate degree in one of these disciplinaries, prospective quants pursue a masters and/or Ph.D. in Financial Engineering or FinTech. In addition to education through a relevant major, achieving a certificate in Chartered Financial Analysis (CFA) and Certificate in Quantitative Finance (CQF) is highly recommended. Although companies won’t require these certifications, quants with these certificates have an competitive edge over other prospective quants. Alongside education, a prospective quant should work in a professional environment through a financial analyst, software developer, or actuary internship.

This is why I have applied, and have been accepted, into multiple colleges to pursue an undergraduate degree in the subjects recommended for quants. I also look to pursue a Ph.D. in FinTech to increase my skillset. While in school, I will simultaneously work on achieving both of the Quant Certificates as well as gaining more industry experience through internships.

Job Outlook

The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) lumps quantitative analysis under the umbrella of financial analysis. They predict the field of financial analysis will grow by 14% from 2012 to 2022. However, CNN Money states that the outlooks for quants specifically is projected to be 41.2% over the next 10 years; as there is an accelerated growth of automated trading systems through hedge funds in the 21st century. Innovation in liquid securities and risk modeling has also opened more job opportunities in quantitative analysis.

Salary

Being in the realm of finance, a quantitative analyst makes a six-figure paycheck, and gains additional compensations, that sometimes are valued more than their annual salary. According to Glassdoor’s World Wide Salary Report, the national average salary for an entry-level quantitative analyst in the United States is $116,183, with an average cash compensation between $5,471 and $100,200. This range represents the entirety of USA, though, as salary ranges greatly differ by region. In San Jose, California, a base quant salary is about $148,616 with compensations as high as $120,000. In the economic capital of USA, New York City, New York, a base salary is $135,551, with compensation going up to $128,198. Because both California and New York are known as two of the most represented financial states in the USA, they have a high salary inflation. In Topeka, Kansas, a state not known for their financial prowess, a base quantitative analyst makes a mere $87,711, which is 25% below the USA average. The United Kingdom is the second best location for quants, as they host one of the largest financial capitals of the world, the London Square Mile. In the UK, the base salary for quants is £88,580, or $115,000.

Location for Masters/Ph.D. education is very important, as base salary varied depending on the school name. Rated by QuantNet, an average Financial Engineering degree from Princeton University leads to an average base salary of $120,000 (excluding compensation), while a degree from lesser ranked Claremont Graduate School receives an average base salary of $62,333.

This, however, is only the base salary for a quant. If a quant stays in a company for more than three years, their pay and compensation increase exponentially. Their salaries can go above $250,00 a year, including an annual compensation package of over $500,000. With experience, a quant also advances to the highest title: Senior Quantitative analyst.

Advancement and Post Careers

All quants start off as Quantitative Analysts, and work as a quant trader, quant researcher, or quant developer. Quantitative analysts start off working in back-office jobs, and can be promoted to a front-office job after showing clear financial talent. After working with a company for over 4 years, they can be promoted to a Senior Quantitative Analyst, the highest quantitative analyst position. The experience a Quantitative Analyst obtains through their work in both technical and business management is fundamental for any type of management, which is why it isn’t uncommon for quants to work in the field, and then move into a managerial role at another company. A quant can use their technical skills to go into software development, becoming a manager and Chief Technology Officer. They can also go through a financial path, becoming a hedge fund manager or a Chief Financial Officer for a company. Because of their jack-of-all-trades characteristics, they can even become entrepreneurs of their own business.

Key Industry Terms

Financial Engineer- Generally known as quantitative analysts, they are tasked with correctly pricing financial products. They do so by using an intensive background in stochastic calculus and Java/C++ coding libraries.

Front Office Quant- A Quantitative Trader who is working one-on-one with traders, designing stock market algorithms and supplying computer-based pricing and trading tools

Back/Middle Office Quant- A Quantitative Modeler or Researcher who is involved in researching and evaluating statistical models or generating new financial strategies.

Alpha- A term to signify that you are able to predict the market better than the average person on the market. Higher the alpha the better a trader you are.

Crowdedness- The theory that if you are the only one using a financial strategy, you are profitable, but if many people use it, it becomes less profitable.

Quantitative Developer- There are two types of developers. The first type works with other quantitative analysts in the Front Office, utilizing MATLAB and Java prototype code to implement and optimize financial models. The second type works in the Back/Middle Office, coding the architecture that is used for front office developer tasks.

Ultra-High Frequency Trading(UHFT)- A developer who masters Unix Network Programming to get the opportunity to work in an secretive environment where trade orders are measured in milliseconds.

Quantitative Trader- A quant specializing in Artificial Intelligence or Applied Mathematics to designs algorithms that search for alpha. These algorithms often have a heavy econometric, statistical or machine-learning background.

Quantitative Researcher- A mathematician that brainstorms endless possibilities to predict future market returns. Rather than implementing financial models, they research new ways to increase their company Alpha.

Quantitative Risk Manager- An analyst who uses Value at Risk (VaR) tests to test various financial assets and markets to lay a secure foundation for quant traders to begin their work.
